
# Preservation

## Purpose

For edits and continuity tasks, explicitly state what must remain unchanged.

Preservation language is the skeleton key for preventing drift.

## Preserve in this order

1. identity / subject geometry
2. crop and camera angle
3. pose and silhouette
4. materials and finishes
5. labels, logos, or exact text
6. shadows and reflections
7. environment composition

## Example 1 — portrait edit

"Preserve the exact face shape, hairline, eye spacing, jacket cut, torso angle, and crop. Do not alter expression or background depth."

## Example 2 — product edit

"Preserve the bottle silhouette, cap alignment, label geometry, front-facing orientation, tabletop reflection, and shadow footprint."

## Example 3 — continuity series

"Keep the mascot helmet shape, visor tint, chest emblem, glove design, and suit seam lines identical across every frame."

## Strong preservation verbs

- preserve
- lock
- keep exact
- maintain
- do not alter
- hold constant

## Weak preservation verbs

- kind of keep
- roughly similar
- mostly the same

That kind of language invites chaos in through the side door.

## Preservation checklist

Before writing the edit prompt, ask:
- what can drift safely?
- what would break the user's goal if it drifted?
- what details are brand-, identity-, or continuity-critical?

## Common mistakes

- preserving only the subject but not the crop,
- preserving crop but forgetting shadow logic,
- saying "same bottle" without naming what makes it the same,
- over-preserving everything so no meaningful change can happen.

Balance is the game.
